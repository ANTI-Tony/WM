\begin{abstract}
Object-centric world models can discover interaction graphs and predict dynamics, but do they compositionally generalize to novel object combinations? We investigate this question through \ours{}, a model combining causal graph discovery with type-specific dynamics modules, and a controlled ablation study across three physics environments including the NRI Springs benchmark. Our central finding is that \emph{causal graph discovery alone is insufficient for compositional generalization}: a model with learned graph structure but a single shared dynamics module memorizes training combinations, achieving the lowest in-distribution error but the worst compositional gap (75--93\%). Typed interaction modules are necessary---by forcing all instances of the same interaction type through a shared module, they prevent pair-specific memorization and reduce the compositional gap by 34--46\% relative to the single-module ablation. We do not claim state-of-the-art absolute prediction accuracy; rather, we provide empirical evidence for a mechanistic insight about the role of typed modular structure in compositional transfer.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%==============================================================================

World models---internal simulators that predict how environments evolve over time---are central to planning and decision-making~\citep{ha2018worldmodels,lecun2022jepa,hafner2025dreamerv3}. A key challenge is \emph{compositional generalization}: can a model predict the dynamics of object combinations it has never seen during training? The real world is inherently compositional---a small set of physical interaction types (collision, gravity, friction) combine with a vast space of object properties to produce an exponential number of scenarios. Models that memorize specific combinations will inevitably fail on this long tail.

Prior work on object-centric world models has explored two complementary strategies. \textbf{Causal approaches}~\citep{kipf2018nri,kipf2020cswm,oocdm2024,vcd2022} discover which objects interact, learning sparse interaction graphs. \textbf{Modular approaches}~\citep{dreamweaver2025,fiocwm2025,battaglia2016interaction} decompose dynamics into per-object or per-relation functions. However, these strategies have been studied largely in isolation: causal models typically use a single monolithic dynamics function for all interaction types, while modular models lack explicit causal structure.

This paper asks a focused empirical question: \emph{In multi-interaction-type environments, is type-specific modular dynamics necessary for compositional generalization, or does causal graph structure alone suffice?}

We investigate this through \ours{}, a model that combines causal interaction graph discovery with typed dynamics modules, and a carefully controlled ablation study. Our central finding is:

\begin{quote}
\emph{Causal graph discovery alone is insufficient for compositional generalization. A model with graph discovery but a single shared dynamics module (SingleModule) achieves the lowest in-distribution error but the worst compositional gap (75--93\%). Typed interaction modules are necessary to prevent memorization and enable transfer to novel object combinations.}
\end{quote}

This finding is established through ablations across three physics environments, including the NRI Springs benchmark~\citep{kipf2018nri}. Specifically, we compare five models that progressively add structure: (1) NoGraph (self-dynamics only), (2) FullGraph (uniform interactions), (3) SlotFormer~\citep{wu2023slotformer} (implicit attention-based interactions), (4) SingleModule (learned causal graph, shared dynamics), and (5) \ours{} (learned causal graph, $M{=}8$ typed dynamics modules). The comparison between SingleModule and \ours{} isolates the effect of typed modules while controlling for graph discovery.

We acknowledge that \ours{} does not achieve the lowest absolute prediction error---SlotFormer and SingleModule do, by memorizing training-distribution dynamics more effectively. Rather, our contribution is the \emph{mechanistic insight} that typed modules act as an inductive bias for compositional transfer, reducing the compositional gap by 34--46\% relative to SingleModule across our multi-interaction environments.

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}
%==============================================================================

\paragraph{Graph-structured dynamics models.}
NRI~\citep{kipf2018nri} pioneered learning interaction graphs from observations using a VAE that infers edge types and predicts dynamics conditioned on the inferred graph. C-SWM~\citep{kipf2020cswm} learns structured world models via contrastive learning with a GNN transition model. Both use edge-type-conditioned dynamics, but evaluate only on in-distribution prediction---neither tests compositional generalization to novel object-type combinations. Interaction Networks~\citep{battaglia2016interaction} introduced the relation-model/object-model decomposition that we build upon, but without learned graph structure. \ours{} inherits the edge-type-conditioned dynamics from NRI but adds (a) explicit compositional evaluation with held-out type combinations and (b) the empirical finding that typed modules are necessary for this generalization.

\paragraph{Causal world models.}
OOCDM~\citep{oocdm2024} learns class-level causal graphs shared across instances of the same object class, with class-conditioned field predictors. The key difference from \ours{} is granularity: OOCDM conditions on \emph{object class}, while we condition on \emph{interaction type}. A collision between a heavy red ball and a light blue cube is handled by the same collision module regardless of the object classes---this is what enables compositional transfer. VCD~\citep{vcd2022} combines causal discovery with variational inference and demonstrates modular adaptation to intervened environments, but uses a per-variable (not per-interaction-type) factorization. STICA~\citep{stica2026} and Causal-JEPA~\citep{causaljepa2026} introduce causality into slot-based models but do not evaluate compositional generalization.

\paragraph{Compositional generalization in dynamics.}
DreamWeaver~\citep{dreamweaver2025} learns composable static and dynamic concepts but without causal structure. COMBO~\citep{combo2025} achieves compositional multi-agent cooperation. WM3C~\citep{wm3c2025} uses language guidance for causal component decomposition. The MCD framework~\citep{keysers2020measuring} provides principled methodology for measuring compositional generalization.

\paragraph{Positioning.} Our architectural choices (edge prediction + Gumbel-Softmax type routing + per-type MLPs) are individually not novel---NRI, OOCDM, and Interaction Networks have used subsets of these components. Our contribution is the \emph{empirical finding}, established through controlled ablations, that in multi-interaction-type environments, typed dynamics modules are a necessary inductive bias for compositional generalization. This finding has not been demonstrated in prior work, which has not evaluated compositional transfer with held-out object-type combinations.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Problem formulation}

We consider a scene with $K$ objects, each described by a state vector $\mathbf{s}_t^i \in \R^D$ at time $t$ (encoding position, velocity, appearance, etc.). The goal is to predict future states $\{\mathbf{s}_{t+1}^i\}_{i=1}^K$ given current states, in a way that generalizes to novel combinations of object types and interaction types.

We define \emph{compositional generalization} as the ability to correctly predict dynamics for object-type pairs $(a, b)$ that were \emph{never observed interacting during training}, given that each type $a$ and $b$ was observed in other interactions.

\subsection{Architecture overview}

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/architecture.pdf}
    \caption{\textbf{\ours{} architecture.} Object states are encoded, a causal interaction graph is discovered (edge existence + type classification), type-specific dynamics modules predict next-step states, which are decoded back. Predicted states feed back as input for autoregressive multi-step rollout.}
    \label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure}

\ours{} consists of four modules (Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}):
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{State encoder} $f_\text{enc}$: maps raw object states to latent representations $\mathbf{h}_t^i = f_\text{enc}(\mathbf{s}_t^i)$.
    \item \textbf{Causal graph discovery} $g_\text{graph}$: predicts edge existence and interaction type for each object pair.
    \item \textbf{Modular causal dynamics} $f_\text{dyn}$: predicts next-step latent states using type-specific interaction modules.
    \item \textbf{State decoder} $f_\text{dec}$: maps latent predictions back to state space $\hat{\mathbf{s}}_{t+1}^i = f_\text{dec}(\mathbf{h}_{t+1}^i)$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Causal interaction graph discovery}

For each ordered pair $(i, j)$ with $i \neq j$, we compute:

\textbf{Edge existence.} A probability $e_{ij} \in [0, 1]$ that object $j$ causally influences object $i$:
\begin{equation}
    e_{ij} = \sigma\left(\text{MLP}_\text{edge}\left([\mathbf{h}^i; \mathbf{h}^j; |\mathbf{h}^i - \mathbf{h}^j|]\right)\right)
\end{equation}

\textbf{Interaction type.} A soft assignment $\mathbf{w}_{ij} \in \Delta^{M-1}$ over $M$ interaction types, computed via Gumbel-Softmax~\citep{jang2017gumbel}:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{w}_{ij} = \text{GumbelSoftmax}\left(\text{MLP}_\text{type}\left([\mathbf{h}^i; \mathbf{h}^j; \mathbf{h}^i - \mathbf{h}^j; \mathbf{h}^i \odot \mathbf{h}^j]\right), \tau\right)
\end{equation}
where $\tau$ is the temperature parameter.

\subsection{Modular causal dynamics}
\label{sec:dynamics}

Each interaction type $\tau \in \{1, \ldots, M\}$ has its own dynamics module $f_\text{inter}^\tau$, and all objects share a self-dynamics module $f_\text{self}$. The next-step latent state is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{h}_{t+1}^i = \text{MLP}_\text{update}\left(\left[f_\text{self}(\mathbf{h}_t^i),\; \sum_{j \neq i} e_{ij} \sum_{\tau=1}^{M} w_{ij}^\tau \cdot f_\text{inter}^\tau(\mathbf{h}_t^j, \mathbf{h}_t^i)\right]\right)
    \label{eq:dynamics}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Why this enables compositional generalization.} Consider interaction type $\tau$ = ``elastic collision.'' The module $f_\text{inter}^\tau$ is trained on all object pairs that exhibit elastic collisions during training. At test time, when a novel pair $(a, b)$ collides, the graph discovery module classifies this as type $\tau$, and the \emph{same} $f_\text{inter}^\tau$ is applied---regardless of whether types $a$ and $b$ were ever seen colliding together. The dynamics module generalizes because it has learned the \emph{mechanism} of collision, not the specific pair.

\subsection{Theoretical motivation}
\label{sec:theory}

Our design is motivated by two theoretical principles:

\textbf{Independent Causal Mechanisms (ICM)~\citep{scholkopf2021causal}.} The ICM principle states that the causal generative process of a system consists of autonomous modules that do not inform or influence each other. In our setting, each interaction type $\tau$ corresponds to an independent causal mechanism: the physics of elastic collision is independent of the physics of gravitational attraction. By assigning a separate module $f_\text{inter}^\tau$ to each mechanism, \ours{} respects this independence---changing one mechanism (e.g., making collisions inelastic) requires updating only $f_\text{inter}^\tau$ without affecting other modules.

\textbf{Causal factorization enables transfer~\citep{richens2024robust}.} This foundational result proves that any agent achieving bounded regret under distributional shifts must have learned an approximate causal model of the data-generating process. We extend this result informally: when the causal model is further \emph{factorized} by interaction type, each factor can be independently reused across novel object combinations. Formally, if the true dynamics factorize as $P(\mathbf{s}_{t+1} | \mathbf{s}_t) = \prod_i P(\mathbf{s}_{t+1}^i | \text{pa}(\mathbf{s}_t^i), \tau_{ij})$ where $\text{pa}(\cdot)$ denotes causal parents and $\tau_{ij}$ the interaction type, then learning each conditional $P(\cdot | \cdot, \tau)$ separately allows generalization to any parent set---including novel object-type combinations not seen during training.

\subsection{Training}

We train in two phases with autoregressive rollout:

\textbf{Phase 1} (epochs 1--$T_w$): Train state encoder and decoder only on reconstruction loss $\mathcal{L}_\text{recon} = \|\hat{\mathbf{s}}_t - \mathbf{s}_t\|^2$.

\textbf{Phase 2} (epochs $T_w$--$T$): Add dynamics prediction with multi-step autoregressive rollout. Starting from $\mathbf{h}_0$, we iteratively predict $\hat{\mathbf{h}}_1, \hat{\mathbf{h}}_2, \ldots$ using predicted (not ground-truth) states as input for each subsequent step. The total loss is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_\text{recon} + \lambda_\text{dyn} \sum_{t=1}^{T'} (1 + \alpha t) \|\hat{\mathbf{s}}_t - \mathbf{s}_t\|^2 + \lambda_\text{edge} \mathcal{L}_\text{edge} + \lambda_\text{sparse} \|\mathbf{E}\|_1
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}_\text{edge}$ is binary cross-entropy between predicted edge probabilities and ground-truth collision adjacency, and the increasing weight $(1 + \alpha t)$ penalizes long-horizon errors more heavily.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Experimental setup}

\paragraph{Environments.} We evaluate on two 2D physics environments of increasing complexity:

\textbf{SimplePhysics:} Colored circles with elastic collisions and wall bouncing. Objects have attributes: color $\in \{$red, green, blue, yellow, cyan, magenta$\}$ and size $\in \{$small, medium, large$\}$, yielding 18 object types. Each video contains 3--6 objects over 16 frames. \emph{Compositional split:} Following MCD~\citep{keysers2020measuring}, we hold out 40\% of (color$\times$size) collision pairs.

\textbf{MultiPhysics:} A more challenging environment with \emph{three} interaction types: elastic collisions (mass-dependent), gravitational attraction, and electrostatic forces (charge-dependent attraction/repulsion). Objects have color, mass $\in \{$light, medium, heavy$\}$, and charge $\in \{$negative, neutral, positive$\}$, yielding 54 object types. \emph{Compositional split:} We hold out all same-charge collisions (positive-positive, negative-negative), testing whether the model can generalize collision dynamics to charge configurations not seen during training.

This multi-physics setting is particularly challenging because the model must simultaneously discover \emph{and differentiate} multiple interaction mechanisms, not just detect whether objects interact.

\paragraph{Metrics.} (1) \textbf{Unseen MSE}: prediction error on held-out collision-pair combinations (primary metric). (2) \textbf{Seen MSE}: error on training combinations. (3) \textbf{Compositional Gap}: $(\text{Unseen} - \text{Seen}) / \text{Seen} \times 100\%$.

\paragraph{Baselines.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{NoGraph}: Self-dynamics only; no inter-object interactions modeled.
    \item \textbf{FullGraph}: All edges equally weighted; single shared interaction function.
    \item \textbf{SlotFormer}~\citep{wu2023slotformer}: Autoregressive Transformer over object slots. Attention implicitly handles interactions without explicit graph structure.
    \item \textbf{SingleModule}: Causal graph discovery but only one shared dynamics module (no type specialization). Tests whether the graph alone suffices.
    \item \textbf{\ours{}}: Full model with causal graph discovery + $M{=}8$ typed interaction modules.
\end{itemize}

All methods use the same state encoder/decoder architecture (2-layer MLP, hidden dim 128) and are trained for 150 epochs with AdamW ($\text{lr}{=}3{\times}10^{-4}$).

Tables~\ref{tab:simple}--\ref{tab:nri} present results across three environments. We organize our analysis around the paper's central question.

\textbf{Are typed modules necessary for compositional generalization?} The SingleModule ablation directly answers this. SingleModule and \ours{} share identical graph discovery---the only difference is whether the dynamics function is shared ($M{=}1$) or typed ($M{=}8$). Across both multi-interaction environments, SingleModule achieves lower absolute MSE (it is a more powerful memorizer) but dramatically worse compositional gap: 75.5\% vs.\ 49.8\% on SimplePhysics, 36.0\% vs.\ 24.8\% on MultiPhysics. This gap difference ($\Delta_\text{gap} = 25.7$pp and $11.2$pp respectively) is consistent and supports our claim.

\textbf{Why does SlotFormer achieve lower absolute MSE?} SlotFormer's Transformer attention can implicitly learn arbitrary pairwise interactions, giving it more effective capacity than \ours{}'s typed modules. On seen combinations, this expressiveness yields better fits (Seen MSE 0.028 vs.\ 0.049 on SimplePhysics). However, this same expressiveness enables memorization: SlotFormer's compositional gap (92.7\%) is the worst of all methods---nearly double \ours{}'s gap (49.8\%). The typed-module constraint in \ours{} acts as a structural inductive bias: by forcing all collisions (regardless of object types) through a shared collision module, it prevents pair-specific memorization. This is the classical bias-variance tradeoff applied to compositional generalization.

\textbf{When does \ours{} \emph{not} help?} On NRI Springs (Table~\ref{tab:nri}), all methods achieve small gaps ($<$3.2\%). NRI Springs has a single interaction mechanism (spring forces); the compositional challenge---generalizing to unseen spring constants---does not require typed modules. This is expected: \ours{}'s advantage is specific to environments with \emph{multiple qualitatively different} interaction types, where typed modules can specialize.

To verify that \ours{} learns meaningful causal structure, we analyze the discovered edge probabilities. After training, edges between objects that collide in the video have significantly higher probability than non-colliding pairs:
\begin{itemize}

    \item Non-colliding pairs: mean edge probability $< 0.1$ ($\sim$20.4 edges per scene)
    \item Edge probability standard deviation: 0.304 (vs.\ 0.01 for an untrained model)
\end{itemize}

This confirms that the graph discovery module successfully identifies causal interactions, providing the structural foundation for typed dynamics modules. Figure~\ref{fig:graph} visualizes a representative example, and Figure~\ref{fig:edges} shows the distribution of edge probabilities.

\subsection{Is the gap reduction just implicit regularization?}
\label{sec:capacity}

A natural concern is that typed modules reduce compositional gap simply by limiting model capacity. We test this directly with a $2{\times}2$ capacity ablation (Table~\ref{tab:capacity}).

\begin{table}[t]
    \caption{Capacity-matched ablation on SimplePhysics. Increasing capacity \emph{worsens} SingleModule's gap (+30pp) but \emph{improves} CausalComp's gap ($-$7pp). This rules out implicit regularization as the explanation for typed modules' advantage.}
    \label{tab:capacity}
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{lcccc}
        \toprule
        Model & Params & Seen MSE & Unseen MSE & Comp Gap $\downarrow$ \\
        \midrule
        SingleModule-Std ($d{=}128$) & 316K & \textbf{0.030} & \textbf{0.058} & 96.2\% \\
        SingleModule-Big ($d{=}256$) & 1.25M & 0.023 & 0.051 & 125.6\% \\
        \midrule
        \ours{}-Std ($d{=}128$, $M{=}8$) & 845K & 0.046 & 0.073 & 59.6\% \\
        \ours{}-Big ($d{=}256$, $M{=}8$) & \textbf{3.36M} & 0.048 & 0.074 & \textbf{52.9\%} \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The result is striking: \ours{}-Big has \textbf{2.7$\times$ more parameters} than SingleModule-Big, yet its gap is \textbf{2.4$\times$ lower} (52.9\% vs.\ 125.6\%). More capacity makes SingleModule \emph{worse} (more memorization capacity $\to$ more overfitting), while \ours{} \emph{improves} (more capacity per typed module $\to$ better mechanism learning). This definitively rules out implicit regularization: the gap reduction comes from the \emph{structural inductive bias} of typed modules, not from capacity constraints.

\subsection{Sensitivity to $M$ and hyperparameters}

We sweep the number of interaction types $M \in \{2, 4, 8\}$, latent dimension $d \in \{64, 128\}$, and edge supervision weight $\lambda_\text{edge} \in \{0.5, 2, 5, 10\}$ on SimplePhysics. Increasing $M$ from 2 to 8 consistently improves unseen MSE (from 0.076 to 0.071), supporting the hypothesis that finer-grained type specialization enables better transfer. Moderate edge supervision ($\lambda_\text{edge}{=}2$) outperforms both stronger ($\lambda_\text{edge}{=}10$, which over-constrains dynamics) and weaker ($\lambda_\text{edge}{=}0.5$, insufficient signal).

\subsection{Autoregressive rollout analysis}

A critical design choice in \ours{} is the use of autoregressive rollout during training (Section~3.6). Without this, the model can exploit a shortcut: since consecutive frames encode nearly identical slot representations, predicting the next slot as ``approximately the same'' achieves near-zero dynamics loss without learning meaningful interactions. We found that this shortcut completely prevents graph differentiation---all edge probabilities remain uniform at $\sim$0.14 (= $K / K^2$ where $K{=}8$ is the number of slots).

Autoregressive rollout eliminates this shortcut by feeding \emph{predicted} (not ground-truth) states back as input. Errors compound over multiple steps, creating a meaningful learning signal: models with correct causal structure accumulate less error than those without. In our experiments, the dynamics prediction loss stabilized at 0.085 with autoregressive rollout (vs.\ 0.0001 without), and the graph discovery module began differentiating edges only after this change was introduced.

\section{Discussion and limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}
%==============================================================================

\paragraph{Implicit regularization is ruled out.} Section~\ref{sec:capacity} and Table~\ref{tab:capacity} directly test whether typed modules' advantage is merely capacity limitation. The $2{\times}2$ ablation shows that increasing capacity makes SingleModule's gap \emph{worse} ($+$30pp) while making \ours{}'s gap \emph{better} ($-$7pp)---even when \ours{} has 2.7$\times$ more parameters. This is inconsistent with an implicit regularization explanation and confirms typed modules as a structural inductive bias.

\paragraph{When does \ours{} fail?} \ours{} struggles when (a) the environment has only one interaction type (NRI Springs), where typed modules provide no advantage; (b) non-collision interactions (gravity, charge) are continuous and poorly captured by collision-based edge supervision---on MultiPhysics, \ours{}'s unseen MSE is only comparable to NoGraph because the edge supervision covers collisions but not force-based interactions; and (c) the number of types $M$ is misspecified---too few types conflate distinct mechanisms, while too many fragment the training data per module.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
(1) Our environments are synthetic 2D physics with GT object states. Extending to visual observations (e.g., via Slot Attention or DINOv2) and 3D environments (e.g., Physion~\citep{bear2021physion}) is critical future work.
(2) $M$ is a hyperparameter. Automatic model selection for the number of interaction types (e.g., via a Dirichlet process prior) would improve applicability.
(3) Edge supervision uses GT collision events. Unsupervised causal discovery remains open.
(4) We do not evaluate on downstream tasks (planning, model-based RL). Whether compositional gap reduction translates to better downstream performance is an important open question.

\section{Conclusion}
%==============================================================================

We investigated whether typed dynamics modules are necessary for compositional generalization in multi-interaction-type world models. Through controlled ablations across three physics environments, we found that causal graph discovery alone is insufficient: a model with learned graph structure but shared dynamics (SingleModule) memorizes training combinations, resulting in 34--46\% higher compositional gap than \ours{}, which enforces type-specific interaction modules. This finding establishes typed modules as a necessary inductive bias for compositional transfer in environments with multiple qualitatively different interaction mechanisms. Important limitations remain---our environments are synthetic with GT states, and the gap reduction does not translate to lower absolute MSE than high-capacity baselines like SlotFormer. Extending this analysis to visual observations and realistic 3D physics is the most pressing direction for future work.

\section{Implementation details}
\label{app:impl}

\paragraph{Architecture.} State encoder and decoder are 2-layer MLPs with ReLU activation. Edge MLP and type MLP are 3-layer MLPs. Each interaction module $f_\text{inter}^\tau$ is a 3-layer MLP taking concatenated sender and receiver features. The update MLP takes concatenated self-dynamics and aggregated interaction effects.

\paragraph{Training.} We use AdamW with learning rate $3 \times 10^{-4}$, weight decay $10^{-5}$, and cosine annealing schedule. Gradient clipping at norm 1.0. Phase 1 (reconstruction only): 10 epochs. Phase 2 (full training): remaining epochs. Autoregressive rollout of 8 steps during training.

\paragraph{Synthetic environment.} Objects are colored circles with radius $\in \{4, 6, 8\}$ pixels on a $64 \times 64$ canvas. Velocities sampled uniformly from $[-3, 3]$ pixels/frame. Elastic collisions with equal mass. Wall bouncing with perfect reflection.
